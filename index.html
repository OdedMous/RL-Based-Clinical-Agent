Abstract

Septic shock is a life-threatening condition that demands timely interventions, particularly in the form of antibiotics, circulatory support via vasopressors and intravenous (IV) fluids. However, due to the complexity of the condition, optimal dosing strategies remain unclear. Reinforcement learning (RL) provides a data-driven approach to address this issue by learning optimal treatment strategies from clinical data. Previous studies have developed RL models for sepsis treatment at coarse, hourly resolutions, focusing on long-term outcomes and neglecting the crucial role of short-term physiological responses in guiding urgent interventions. In this paper, we describe a novel, one minute-by-minute RL model that includes attention-based neural architecture to capture the complex and evolving temporal patterns of patient clinical dynamics, while optimizing a new reward function that emphasizes immediate hemodynamic stabilization. Empirical evaluation on two ICU datasets demonstrate that our method significantly outperforms several baseline approaches, including the clinicians’ policy, rule-based policies, and a supervised learning-based policy, in terms of the estimated return. Moreover, our model exhibited risk-aware behavior, adjusting treatment intensity based on patient risk level. Attention weights analysis revealed that different layers focus on distinct time frames, and that the attention patterns alone were highly predictive of patient risk, highlighting the model’s effective use of temporal information.

Motivation

Process of Sepsis: Sepsis occurs when an infection causes blood vessels to leak, reducing oxygen supply to organs. In severe cases, this may result in septic shock, a life-threatening drop in blood pressure and multiple organ dysfunction.

Treatment: Treatment for sepsis in the ICU focuses on three main components: blood pressure stabilization using two types of medications (vasopressors and IV fluids), supportive care, and infection control. In this work, we focus on the first component - optimizing medication scheduling and dosing levels.

Challenges: Treating sepsis is difficult because vasopressors and IV fluids act quickly, affect multiple organs, and have varying effects across patients, while the disease progresses rapidly. These challenges often lead to ineffective treatment, highlighting the need for improved strategies.

Problem Formulation

We frame sepsis management as a reinforcement learning problem, in which the agent learns to choose treatment actions that stabilize the patient’s hemodynamic state.

State (S): A vector that represents the patient's demographic information and physiological status, including around 40 different variables.

Action (A): A combination of vasopressor and IV fluid doses, selected from 36 possible options, including a “zero dose” (no drug).

Reward (R): A scalar value providing feedback on treatment quality. In our case, it reflects achieving the target blood pressure (BP = 65) and reducing lactate levels.

Patient Trajectory: A sequence of states, actions, and rewards over time, representing the treatment progression.

Model Architeture & Policy Learning

We train an attention-based Q-network to predict action values from patient states using past recorded trajectories from real patients. We use clinical data with a 1-minute resolution to capture rapid physiological changes, unlike previous studies that use 1–4 hour intervals.

The Q-network takes as input a sequence of recent patient states and processes them using transformer encoder layers, allowing it to capture how the patient’s condition evolves over time.

Results

Agent Performance Comparison

We evaluated our RL agent against five other agents: Clinicians, Zero-Dose, Max-Dose, Random-Dose, and Supervised Learning (SL). Our RL agent achieved the highest median return, demonstrating its superior treatment performance.

Clinicians vs RL decision-making analysis

We analyzed how clinicians and our RL agent make treatment decisions across patient risk levels, by examining their action histogram matrices, where each cell represents the proportion (%) of actions taken for a specific combination of VPs (x-axis) and IV fluid doses (y-axis).

The RL agent, like the Clinicians, exhibits risk-aware behavior, administering higher doses for high-risk patients. However, for these high-risk states, it consistently provides some medication, rarely selecting zero-dose actions, unlike clinicians who sometimes withhold treatment.
